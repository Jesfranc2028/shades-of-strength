Title: The Girl with the Sun-Kissed Skin

When I was ten, a pale patch appeared on my left hand small, harmless, like a cloud had settled on my skin. I didn’t think much of it. But over the weeks, more clouds gathered. First on my hands, then my face, then my knees. I watched in silence, hoping no one would notice. But Nairobi isn’t a city where you can hide easily, especially when your skin starts to change color.

At school, whispers followed me like shadows. “Is it contagious?” “Is she sick?” I didn’t have answers. Back then, vitiligo was a stranger...

My mother thought it was a rash. An aunt thought I had been cursed. A friend’s parent told her to stop playing with me. I started wearing long sleeves, even in the heat, and avoided mirrors. Some of my family didn’t know how to talk to me anymore — some didn’t try. The rejection stung more than the sun.

Years passed. I grew older, quieter, more withdrawn. Then one day, when I was about fifteen, I typed my symptoms into an internet café computer. Vitiligo. The word felt foreign and familiar all at once...

It took years to make peace with my reflection. Some days are still hard. But now, I wear short sleeves when I want. I walk in the sun carefully, but not fearfully. I take care of my skin and, more importantly, my spirit.

To anyone struggling with vitiligo: you are not alone. There’s a quiet community of warriors like you learning to see the beauty in the difference, and the strength in the journey.

And if one day, you see a girl walking confidently through the streets of Nairobi, her skin kissed by the sun in places it no longer colors know that she’s not hiding anymore.
